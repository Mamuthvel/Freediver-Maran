Freediving is the practice of diving underwater on a single breath of air. No tank, no regulator, no bubbles — just you, your lungs, and the water. It is simultaneously one of humanity's oldest activities (pearl and sponge divers have done it for millennia) and one of its fastest-growing modern sports.

## How is that even possible?

Your body already knows how. Every human carries the **mammalian dive response**: submerge your face in water and your heart rate drops, blood shifts toward your core, and your oxygen consumption falls. Freediving training doesn't fight your physiology — it wakes it up.

With a single weekend course, most complete beginners achieve:

- A static breath-hold of 90 seconds to 2 minutes
- Comfortable dives to 10–16 metres
- A completely different relationship with water

## The disciplines

- **Static apnea** — holding your breath while floating still
- **Dynamic apnea** — swimming horizontally in a pool on one breath
- **Constant weight** — diving down and back along a line, the classic depth discipline
- **Free immersion** — pulling yourself down the line by hand

## Is it dangerous?

Untrained and alone: yes. Trained and with a buddy: remarkably safe. Every certified course drills one unbreakable rule — *never freedive alone* — plus the rescue skills to back it up. Read our full piece on [freediving safety](/blog/how-safe-is-freediving) for the honest numbers.

## How do I start in India?

A one-day [Basic Freediver course](/courses/basic-freediver) gets you in the pool; the two-to-three-day [PADI Freediver course](/courses/padi-freediver) takes you to the open sea and an internationally recognised certification. Warm water and short travel distances make [Pondicherry](/locations/pondicherry) the easiest starting point in the country.
