Eight years of teaching means we've seen every beginner mistake hundreds of times. Here are the seven that matter, in the order they usually appear.

## 1. Hyperventilating before the dive

It feels like preparation; it's actually the most dangerous habit in the sport. It removes your urge to breathe without adding oxygen. **Fix:** slow, relaxed breathe-up — long exhales, no gasping.

## 2. Forcing equalization

Pushing through ear pain causes barotrauma and ends courses early. **Fix:** equalize early, often, and *before* discomfort. If your ears won't clear, ascend a metre and try again. Read our [equalization guide](/blog/equalization-guide).

## 3. Lifting the head to look down the line

Tilting your head extends your neck, breaks your streamline, and makes equalizing harder. **Fix:** tuck the chin; trust the line.

## 4. Kicking too hard

Sprinting burns oxygen exactly when you can't afford it. **Fix:** slow, wide, relaxed fin strokes from the hip — the correct pace feels almost lazy.

## 5. Diving over-weighted

Extra lead makes descent easy and the ascent — the part that matters — dangerous. **Fix:** weight for neutral buoyancy around 10 metres; your instructor will dial this in.

## 6. Treating contractions as an emergency

The first diaphragm contraction is a signal, not a fire alarm. **Fix:** trained divers turn well before contractions become intense; courses teach exactly where your line is.

## 7. Skipping the recovery breathing

The seconds after surfacing are when blackout risk peaks. **Fix:** hook breathing — quick inhale, brief hold, passive exhale, three times — before anything else, every dive, forever.

All seven are drilled out of you inside a [PADI Freediver course](/courses/padi-freediver). That's rather the point of one.
