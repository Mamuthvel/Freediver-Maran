The best-kept secret of freediving: **animals treat you differently.** No bubbles, no motor noise, no hovering wall of neoprene — just a quiet shape moving like they do. Encounters that scuba divers chase, freedivers simply have.

## Pondicherry & the Bay of Bengal

Our [home reefs](/locations/pondicherry) host lionfish holding station in the current, morays peering from the artificial reef modules, octopus doing their liquid-shapeshift routine, and — in season — eagle rays gliding the sand flats. Not postcard-famous, but rich, close, and reliably there.

## Goa & the Arabian Sea

[Grande Island's](/locations/goa) post-monsoon plankton blooms pull in the hunters: barracuda in rotating silver towers, trevally strafing baitballs, and turtles grazing the rocky coves. Nudibranch fans, bring your eyes — the macro life here is superb.

## The Andamans

[Havelock](/locations/andaman) is the headline act. Hawksbill and green turtles on nearly every session, blacktip and whitetip reef sharks patrolling the outer walls, Napoleon wrasse the size of coffee tables, and hard-coral gardens that begin two metres below the surface. The Andamans also hold one of India's last dugong populations — seeing one is rare, and nobody who has forgets it.

## The Maldives

Two encounters justify the [expedition](/locations/maldives) alone. **Manta rays** at cleaning stations barrel-roll within arm's length of breath-hold divers — the stations sit at 10–15 m, as if designed for us. And **whale sharks** cruise South Ari year-round: swimming beside an eight-metre fish on one breath is, our students report, a permanent memory.

## The freediver's contract

Take the encounter, leave everything else: no touching, no chasing, no cornering, weights off the coral. The animals come closer to freedivers precisely because we're quiet — keep the bargain. More on this in our conservation work with local reef-restoration projects, which every Sea Critter course fee helps fund.
