Good news first: freediving is one of the cheapest ocean sports to sustain. After certification, your entire kit fits in a fin bag and needs no refills, no boat, no rentals. Here's what actually matters.

## The essentials

### Low-volume mask (₹2,500–₹6,000)
Freediving masks have small internal air spaces so they cost less air to equalize at depth. A scuba mask works for your course; buy low-volume when you buy your own. Fit test: press it to your face without the strap and inhale gently through the nose — it should hold on its own.

### Long-blade fins (₹6,000–₹25,000)
Long blades convert slow, oxygen-cheap kicks into real propulsion. Plastic blades are perfect for your first two years; carbon can wait until your technique deserves it (and it will tell you when).

### Simple snorkel (₹800–₹2,000)
No valves, no purge, no flex sections. Freedivers remove the snorkel before descending, so simplicity wins.

## Strongly recommended

### Open-cell wetsuit (₹8,000–₹18,000)
Indian water is warm, but 60–90 minute sessions chill anyone, and cold wrecks relaxation. A 3 mm open-cell two-piece is the regional sweet spot.

### Rubber weight belt + lead (₹2,500)
Rubber stays put during dives; nylon scuba belts slide. Your instructor sets your correct weighting — never copy someone else's.

## Later, when depth arrives

- **Freediving computer** (₹18,000+) — depth, dive time, surface intervals, alarms
- **Lanyard** — required for line diving beyond beginner depths
- **Buoy and line** — the training platform itself; usually the club owns one

**Total for a complete personal starter kit: roughly ₹20,000–₹35,000** — about one nice weekend trip, for equipment that lasts a decade. All gear is included in [Sea Critter courses](/courses), so try everything before buying anything.
