Freediving looks extreme, so people assume it's reckless. The truth is more specific: **the sport is safe; the shortcuts are not.** Nearly all serious freediving accidents share two ingredients — no training and no buddy.

## The main risk: hypoxic blackout

Push a breath-hold too far and the brain can shut down from low oxygen — usually in the last few metres of ascent or at the surface, where pressure drop lowers oxygen partial pressure fastest. Sounds terrifying; here's the crucial part: **a blacked-out diver whose airway is protected by a buddy recovers in seconds, typically with no lasting harm.** A solo diver has no one to do that. This single fact is why the buddy rule is absolute.

## How training removes the risk

- **Conservative limits** — certified divers turn early and progress gradually; blackout at recreational limits with proper technique is rare
- **No hyperventilation** — courses eliminate the habit that silently deletes your warning system
- **Direct supervision** — one-on-one attention during every breath-hold, at ratios never exceeding 1:4 at Sea Critter
- **Rescue skills** — every PADI Freediver graduate can recover a buddy from depth and manage them at the surface; we drill it until it's boring

## Secondary risks, managed

Ear barotrauma (prevented by patient equalization), lung squeeze (prevented by conservative depth progression — relevant mainly at advanced depths), and surface hazards like boats (managed with buoys and flags). All curriculum, all trainable.

## The honest comparison

Trained, buddied recreational freediving has an incident rate comparable to mainstream ocean sports. The headlines you've read almost invariably involve solo, untrained breath-hold — which is to freediving what free solo climbing is to a via ferrata.

The safest first step is the supervised one: a [course](/courses) where every hold is watched from day one.
