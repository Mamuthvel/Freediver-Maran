PADI is the world's largest diver-training organisation, and its freediving program is the most widely recognised certification ladder in the sport. Here's how it works, level by level.

## Why certification matters

A certification card is not a diploma for your wall — it's an access key. Dive centres worldwide require proof of training before letting you join sessions, rent equipment, or board boats. A PADI rating earned in [Pondicherry](/locations/pondicherry) works in Dahab, Bali, and the Bahamas.

## The ladder

### PADI Basic Freediver — 1 day, ₹8,500
Pool-only introduction: theory, breathwork, static and dynamic apnea. Credits fully toward the next level. [Details →](/courses/basic-freediver)

### PADI Freediver — 2–3 days, ₹18,500
The full entry certification: eLearning, pool skills, and open-water dives to 16 metres, plus buddy rescue. This is the card that unlocks freediving worldwide. [Details →](/courses/padi-freediver)

### PADI Advanced Freediver — 3–4 days, ₹24,500
Free-fall, refined Frenzel, mouthfill introduction, rescue from 15 m, and depths to 24 metres. [Details →](/courses/advanced-freediver)

### PADI Master Freediver — 4–5 days, ₹34,500
The summit of recreational training: mouthfill to depth, FRC training, 3:30+ statics, and 40-metre dives. Gateway to the instructor pathway. [Details →](/courses/master-freediver)

## Common questions

**Does certification expire?** No — PADI ratings are for life, though after a long break any good centre will suggest a refresher.

**Can I skip levels?** No, and you wouldn't want to: each level's physiology genuinely requires the previous one's adaptations.

**PADI vs AIDA vs Molchanovs?** All three are legitimate; skills cross over and centres recognise equivalents. PADI's advantage is sheer global footprint — more centres honour it, in more places, than any other card.
