Beginners assume breath-hold is about lung capacity. It isn't. It is about **oxygen consumption** and **CO₂ tolerance** — and both are trainable by anyone.

## Why you feel the urge to breathe

The urge to breathe is triggered by rising carbon dioxide, not falling oxygen. When you feel the first contraction of your diaphragm, your blood typically still carries plenty of usable O₂. Training teaches your body to stay relaxed through that signal instead of panicking at it.

## The breathe-up

Before a hold, spend two to three minutes in slow diaphragmatic breathing:

- Inhale gently through the mouth for about 5 seconds, belly first
- Exhale twice as long as you inhale, lips slightly pursed
- Keep shoulders, jaw, and hands deliberately loose
- Finish with one full (not strained) inhalation

**Never hyperventilate.** Rapid deep breathing before a hold doesn't add oxygen — it strips CO₂, deletes your urge to breathe, and is the classic cause of shallow-water blackout.

## Train on dry land

CO₂ tables — repeated holds with shrinking rests — build tolerance safely on your sofa. Two sessions a week is plenty. Combine with daily relaxation practice: a tense body burns oxygen; a calm one sips it.

## The safety line

Dry training: fine alone, seated. **Any breath-hold in water — including your bathtub — requires a trained buddy watching you.** No exceptions, ever. A [Basic Freediver course](/courses/basic-freediver) teaches supervised technique in one day and will typically double your comfortable hold before dinner.
